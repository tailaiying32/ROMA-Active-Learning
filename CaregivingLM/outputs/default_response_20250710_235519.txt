Response:
The key principles of stroke rehabilitation, as derived from the provided context in "Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy," encompass a comprehensive, multifaceted approach tailored to the individual needs of the stroke survivor. These principles focus on addressing both the physical and cognitive challenges faced after a stroke, as well as providing psychosocial support to the individual and their families. The overarching goal of stroke rehabilitation is to enhance the stroke survivor's quality of life by maximizing their functional independence, participation in meaningful activities, and reintegration into their community. Below, these principles are outlined in more detail:

1. **Individualized Care Planning:** Stroke rehabilitation must be customized to meet the unique needs, abilities, and goals of each individual. This involves a thorough assessment of the individual's physical, cognitive, and emotional status and consideration of their personal goals for recovery (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

2. **Multidisciplinary Approach:** Successful stroke rehabilitation involves a team of healthcare professionals, including occupational therapists, physical therapists, speech-language pathologists, nurses, doctors, and psychologists. This team collaborates to address the wide range of challenges that stroke survivors face (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

3. **Focus on Functional Independence:** Rehabilitation activities are centered on improving the individual's ability to perform activities of daily living (ADLs) such as dressing, eating, bathing, and mobility tasks. This includes teaching compensatory strategies when full recovery of function is not possible (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

4. **Incorporation of Adaptive Techniques and Equipment:** When necessary, individuals are taught to use adaptive equipment or assistive devices, such as reachers, button hooks, and specially designed utensils, to enhance their independence in daily tasks (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

5. **Cognitive and Perceptual Rehabilitation:** Interventions address cognitive impairments such as memory, attention, problem-solving, and perceptual deficits. Cognitive retraining and compensatory strategies are employed to help individuals manage these challenges (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

6. **Psychosocial Support:** Emotional and social support is crucial for stroke survivors and their families. Rehabilitation involves addressing emotional well-being, coping strategies, and social reintegration. This includes managing psychological responses such as depression, anxiety, and emotional lability (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

7. **Education:** Providing education to the stroke survivor and their family about stroke, its consequences, and the process of rehabilitation is essential for fostering understanding, setting realistic expectations, and supporting active participation in the recovery process (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

8. **Prevention of Secondary Complications:** Monitoring for and addressing potential secondary complications early, such as contractures, skin breakdown, and depression, is critical to the overall success of rehabilitation (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

9. **Continuous Evaluation and Adjustment:** Rehabilitation plans should be regularly reviewed and adjusted based on the individual's progress and emerging needs to ensure that the rehabilitation goals remain relevant and achievable (Source: Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy).

In summary, the principles of stroke rehabilitation outlined in "Quick Reference to Occupational Therapy" emphasize an individualized, multidisciplinary approach centered on enhancing functional independence, cognitive functioning, and emotional well-being. Through personalized care plans, the use of adaptive strategies and equipment, and the provision of education and psychosocial support, rehabilitation aims to maximize the quality of life for stroke survivors.
